Adjustable rate

An adjustable rate refers to a type of interest rate on a loan that may change periodically based on an index or benchmark interest rate.

Description

In the context of the Indian real estate market, an adjustable rate typically pertains to home loans or mortgages where the interest rate is not fixed for the entire duration of the loan. Instead, it is tied to a benchmark interest rate, such as the Marginal Cost of Funds based Lending Rate (MCLR) or the Repo Rate. This means that the interest rate can fluctuate at predetermined intervals (e.g., annually or semi-annually), which in turn affects the monthly repayments made by the borrower. Borrowers may initially benefit from lower rates at the start of the loan; however, as rates rise, their payments can increase significantly. This type of loan can be appealing in a declining or stable interest rate environment but poses risks in a rising rate scenario, making it essential for borrowers to understand the terms and potential implications of adjustable rate loans.

Examples

  • A home loan with an initial fixed rate for the first three years, after which it adjusts annually based on the MCLR.
  • A personal loan that starts with a low interest rate but adjusts every six months based on the prevailing market rates.

Additional Information

  • Borrowers should carefully consider their financial situation and market conditions when opting for an adjustable rate loan.
  • Most lenders provide a cap on how much the interest rate can increase at each adjustment period, as well as a lifetime cap on the total increase.
